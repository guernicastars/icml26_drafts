\begin{abstract}
We present a three-tier empirical validation of the global convergence
guarantee established in the companion paper.
Theorem~3 states that \MAPG{} equipped with the restart mechanism of
\citet{Giannou2022} converges \emph{almost surely} to a stable meta-Nash
equilibrium, with finite expected restart count and Pareto-optimal selection
among multiple equilibria.
We test this claim on (i)~matrix games with analytically known Nash
equilibria, (ii)~coordinated continuous control in a two-agent HalfCheetah
environment, and (iii)~pluralistic LLM debate using Llama-3.3-70B.
In matrix games, restarts are the \emph{only} mechanism
achieving non-zero global convergence rates, reaching \textbf{97\%} success
in Stag Hunt versus \textbf{0\%} for both REINFORCE and vanilla \MAPG{}.
The continuous-control and LLM results reveal a critical
threshold-sensitivity phenomenon: miscalibrated restart thresholds cause
pathological over-restarting, motivating adaptive restart schedules as an
important open problem.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%=======================================================================

The companion paper proves \textbf{Theorem~3} for \OUR{}:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textit{Local convergence:} starting within a neighbourhood
    $\mathcal{U}^{\mathrm{meta}}$ of a stable meta-Nash, the iterates
    converge almost surely.
  \item \textit{Global convergence via restarts:} the restarted process
    converges a.s.\ to a stable meta-Nash from \emph{arbitrary}
    initialisation, with finite expected restart count and Pareto-optimal
    limit selection (Proposition~4.3 of~\citealt{Giannou2022}).
\end{enumerate}

The \emph{global} part is the novel empirical claim: standard REINFORCE and
vanilla \MAPG{} get trapped in Pareto-inferior Nash basins; the restart
mechanism is the only means of escape.
This report quantifies that escape, identifies a threshold-calibration
phenomenon in continuous domains, and connects both findings to the
three lemmas (bias, variance, meta-GDP/SOS) that underpin Theorem~3.

All matrix-game and continuous-control experiments run on a single
NVIDIA RTX~3060 (6\,GB VRAM).
LLM debate experiments run on 4$\times$V100 (32\,GB each).

\section{Experiment I: Matrix Games}
\label{sec:matrix}
%=======================================================================

\subsection{Setup}

Three algorithms are compared across two games with multiple Nash equilibria.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=3pt]
  \item[Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma (IPD).]
    $R(C,C)=3$,\ $R(D,D)=1$,\ $R(C,D)=0$,\ $R(D,C)=5$.
    Two pure Nash: $(D,D)$ (risk-dominant, payoff~1)
    and $(C,C)$ (Pareto-optimal, payoff~3).
    Agents are initialised with a bias toward Defect.

  \item[Stag Hunt.]
    $R(S,S)=5$,\ $R(H,H)=3$,\ $R(S,H)=0$,\ $R(H,S)=1$.
    Two pure Nash: $(H,H)$ (risk-dominant, payoff~3)
    and $(S,S)$ (payoff-dominant/Pareto-optimal, payoff~5).
\end{description}

\noindent
Each algorithm runs for $1{,}000$ episodes across \textbf{100~seeds}.
All matrix-game experiments use \emph{exact expected-value gradients}
(not sampled rollouts), isolating the effect of the gradient computation
from sampling variance.
Convergence is declared when both agents' cooperation probability exceeds
$0.95$, consistent with the Pareto-optimal Nash.
The restart threshold is set between the sub-optimal and optimal Nash payoffs
($\tau_\text{IPD}=2.5$,\, $\tau_\text{Stag}=4.0$).

\subsection{Algorithm Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:matrix_convergence} summarises the results;
Figure~\ref{fig:algo_comparison} visualises the convergence rates.
Among seeds that converge, the mean first-passage episode is
\textbf{350} (IPD) and \textbf{294} (Stag Hunt), consistent with the
finite-restart bound of Proposition~4.1 in~\citet{Giannou2022}.

We extend to a continuous multi-agent control problem:
two-agent HalfCheetah (\repo{env/halfcheetah.py}).
Agent~0 controls front joints; Agent~1 controls rear joints; both share one
scalar reward.
The sub-optimal local Nash corresponds to mismatched hopping gaits

\noindent
\textit{Configuration:} 4 seeds, 1\,000 episodes, RTX~3060.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*),itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Baseline} --- restarts disabled ($\tau = -9999$).

    (matching \citealt{Giannou2022}, \S4), $\tau=-280$, window~$=100$

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Analysis: The Discrimination Problem}
\label{sec:mujoco_discussion}

\begin{remark}[Exploration--exploitation indistinguishability]
\label{rem:discrimination}
In matrix games, the sub-optimal Nash payoff and the exploration payoff are
\emph{clearly separated} (e.g., $(H,H)=3$ vs.\ random play $\approx 2.75$
in Stag Hunt), allowing a threshold to distinguish them.
In HalfCheetah, the sub-optimal hopping gait ($\approx -350$) produces
rewards \emph{indistinguishable} from early exploration ($\approx -375$).
No fixed threshold~$\tau$ can discriminate ``agent is stuck in a bad Nash''
from ``agent is still learning.''

This is a structural limitation absent from the tabular setting of
\citet{Giannou2022}.
It suggests that, for deep continuous control, the restart detector must
incorporate a {\em temporal} signal (e.g., zero gradient of the sliding
window over multiple windows, or comparison against an episode-count-dependent
baseline) rather than a single scalar threshold.
\end{remark}

  the global restart mechanism triggers \textbf{148 times} (roughly once
  every 5.5 rounds). This frequent wiping of the LoRA adapters traps the
  performance near $-4.0$.
}
\label{fig:llm_progression}
\end{figure}

\begin{remark}[The Necessity of Adaptive Thresholds]
The LLM results perfectly mirror the continuous-control results from
Section~\ref{sec:mujoco}.
In both domains, setting a fixed threshold $\tau$ that is disconnected from
the \emph{running mean} of the reward distribution causes pathological
over-restarting.
This provides powerful empirical evidence that transferring the restart
guarantees of \citet{Giannou2022} from tabular games to deep continuous
settings strictly requires an adaptive threshold parameter $\tau_n$ that
relaxes alongside the bias schedule $K_n$.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=======================================================================

The matrix-game experiments confirm the central claim of Theorem~3(ii):
the global restart mechanism is the \emph{only} component achieving non-zero
convergence to Pareto-optimal Nash equilibria in games with multiple stable
Nash.
The 97\% / 24\% split between Stag Hunt and IPD is theoretically
interpretable via basin geometry: the Stag Hunt risk-dominant basin
$(H,H)$ is shallower relative to the Pareto-optimal $(S,S)$, so fewer
restarts suffice.

The continuous-control (MuJoCo) and LLM debate (Llama-3.3-70B) results
reveal that \emph{threshold calibration} is a first-class design problem
when applying the Giannou restart framework to deep-learning domains.
In both settings, a fixed threshold disconnected from the running reward
distribution caused pathological over-restarting (27--32 restarts in MuJoCo;
148 in LLM debate), trapping performance below the no-restart baseline.
This motivates the design of \emph{adaptive threshold schedules}
$\tau_n$ that relax alongside the bias budget $K_n$ — an important open
problem connecting Lemma~2 of the companion paper to practical algorithm
design.
